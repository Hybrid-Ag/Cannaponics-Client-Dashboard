CRITICAL

Low pH — All blocks 5.54–5.80 vs 6.50+ target. 100% below optimal. Restricts P, K, Ca availability, promotes Al solubility. Cannabis is highly pH-sensitive during flowering.
Low Sulfur — Avg 4.9 ppm vs 10 minimum. 100% deficient. Cannabis S demand 20–40 kg/ha. Current soil supply <2 kg/ha.
Aluminium Toxicity — Block 5 subsoil: 12.6% exch. Al (toxic >5%). Root growth chemically restricted at depth.
Low ECEC (subsoils) — Blocks 3 & 5 ECEC 1.32–1.55 cmol/kg. Extremely low nutrient holding capacity.
Calcium Oversupply — Ca dominates ~80% of cation exchange capacity. Excess Ca may tie up manganese availability. Tissue testing will confirm the extent of the issue, but at 80% CEC there is more than enough calcium to work with.

WATCH

High PBI (P Fixation) — PBI 35–132. Blocks 4–5 extreme (99–132). Standard P fertiliser locks up within 24–48 hours.
Potassium — Avg 49.8 ppm vs 100+ minimum. 80% deficient. Cannabis flowering K demand >100 kg/ha.
Magnesium — Avg 51.4 ppm. Blocks 3, 5 severely deficient (18–20 ppm). Ca:Mg ratio 8.2 in Block 4.
Boron — Avg 0.18 ppm vs 0.5 minimum. 80% deficient. Critical for flower set and cannabinoid synthesis.

ADEQUATE

Key Findings — Climate & Season Traffic Light

10-year climate analysis (2016–2025) at Collie Burn WA — implications for cannabis cultivation and bud development
CRITICAL — Climate Risk
High rainfall and bud rot risk — Collie Burn averages 577 mm annual rain, 102% higher than Davenport (285 mm). Growing season rainfall (Apr–Nov) averages 489 mm vs 215 mm. Wet conditions during flowering (Jun–Aug) create severe humidity stress and powdery mildew/botrytis risk. Extended drying time on buds requires strategic canopy management and potentially early harvest before late-winter rains saturate flowers.
Frost damage to young plants — Coldest night on record was 0.4°C (July 2025). Multiple years show July/August mins below 7°C. Spring frosts during germination (Sep–Oct) can kill seedlings. Autumn frosts (May/early Jun) damage tender growth. Cold tolerance cultivars essential; risk frost cloth or shade during critical weeks.
Extreme seasonal wetness variability — GSR ranges from 389 mm (2023) to 657 mm (2021). A 1.7× swing. Two wet seasons (2021, 2020) delivered over 530 mm during grow; drought years like 2023 (389 mm) compress water availability. Unpredictable rainfall makes irrigation timing critical for bud development nutrient uptake.
WATCH — Seasonal Patterns
Winter-dominant rainfall — 70% of annual rain falls Jun–Aug (avg ~286 mm). This is the grow phase, which is favourable for vegetative growth and early flowering. However, sustained wetness during bud maturation (Jul–Aug) compresses the dry-down window and invites fungal disease. Target defoliation and canopy airflow for Jul–Aug to prevent bud rot during peak bloom.
Late-season moisture stress — September onwards averages just 52.9 mm, a sharp drop. If outdoor cannabis is grown to Oct–Nov (extended season), late-season drying stress can lock up nutrient availability and potassium mobility, reducing resin production and cannabinoid yield.
Humidity during critical windows — June–August avg min temps of 6–7.5°C with 50–110 mm monthly rain. High RH is nearly guaranteed. Cannabis flowering during this window demands vigilant spray schedules for mildew and botrytis, especially in weeks 6–8 of flowering when buds are densest.
FAVOURABLE — Opportunities
GDD accumulation suitable for indoor-trained cannabis — Total annual GDD consistently 5,700–6,280. Cannabis requires 3,000–4,500 GDD for full flower cycle (10–14 weeks). Even in cool years (2025: 6,040), thermal accumulation is adequate for flowering under field conditions, though indoor/controlled environments ensure consistent ripeness.
Consistent cool nights support cannabinoid synthesis — Average min temps 6.6–10.6°C during growing season favour anthocyanin and cannabinoid accumulation. Cooler nights (vs high-heat regions) reduce oxidative stress and support sustained resin production. Cultivars bred for cool climates (e.g. European genetics) will thrive.
Good recovery potential in wet years — 2021 (780 mm), 2020 (590 mm), 2022 (577 mm) delivered excellent rainfall. In recovery years, soil moisture is ample for flowering stretch and nutrient uptake, supporting 25–30% higher yields if foliar nutrition and disease control are tight. Budget major crop for wet years; plan deficit strategy for dry ones.

Climate Observations & Nutrition Implications
Wet Climate & Disease Pressure

Collie Burn's 577 mm avg annual rainfall is extreme by WA standards — double the Pithara wheatbelt. Growing season rainfall (489 mm Apr–Nov) dominates June–August when cannabis is in veg–early flower transition. Sustained wet conditions drive powdery mildew, botrytis cinerea, and damping-off risk on seedlings. Fungicide rotation and canopy airflow are non-negotiable. Plan for weekly preventive sprays (e.g., potassium bicarbonate, sulphur, copper oxychloride) during Jun–Aug. Strategic early harvest (mid-September) avoids late-winter rains saturating flowers.

Frost Risk & Young Plant Protection

July and August min temps average 6.9–7.2°C. Coldest night on record (0.4°C, July 2025) shows frost is real risk. Cannabis seedlings and young transplants are killed by hard frost. Spring frost events (May–Jun) damage tender vegetative growth; autumn frost (late Sep–Oct) can surprise late-harvest crops. Cultivar selection toward cold-hardy genotypes essential. Employ frost cloth during critical weeks (first 4 weeks post-plant in April). For extended fall harvest, time crop to finish by mid-Sep before cold snaps intensify.

Nutrient Uptake During Wet Periods & Foliar Strategy

The Jun–Aug rainfall peak (average 287 mm) is the grow's critical nutrient uptake window. However, high soil moisture and cool temps reduce nutrient cycling speed. Phosphorus availability declines in wet, cool conditions (less microbial mineralization). Foliar feeding becomes primary delivery route: weekly NPK 10-6-20 during veg (May–Jun), then 5-8-20 during bud formation (Jul–Aug). Pair foliar program with root feeding via liquid fish or seaweed extract weekly. In wet years (2020, 2021 >530mm GSR), tissue test at week 6 and week 10 to identify deficiencies early; adjust foliar formulation if P, K, or Mg status lag. In dry years (2023: 389mm), reduce spray frequency to avoid nutrient leaching.
